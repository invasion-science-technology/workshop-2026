University of California, Davis · June 2026

Promoting the use of data-driven research and AI for controlling and managing invasive weeds

An expert workshop to develop a unified framework for collecting and analysing data on invasive plants—bringing together scientists to explore the role of data, models, and AI in mapping and managing invasive weeds, and to identify gaps and opportunities for the field.

  • When June 15–16, 2026 (2 days)
    Pre-workshop icebreaker: June 14
  • Where UC Davis, California, USA
  • Format In-person with remote participation options
View agenda Request to participate

Scope

Invasive species are among the most significant threats to global agriculture and natural ecosystems, with enormous economic and ecological costs. This workshop addresses that challenge by convening pioneers in invasive species research, data science, and agricultural technology for intensive collaboration over two days.

Sessions will explore cutting-edge methodologies, identify research gaps, and develop actionable frameworks for invasive weed management—including predictive capabilities, policy integration, and scalable approaches to detection, monitoring, and management.

Who should engage

  • Agricultural researchers in weed science and crop protection
  • Data scientists and AI specialists interested in agricultural applications
  • Policy makers in agricultural and environmental regulation
  • Extension professionals connecting research to practice
  • Graduate students and postdocs in relevant fields

Registration

Participation is invite-only. If you would like to take part or contribute, please use the contact form so the organising committee can follow up.

A more detailed programme will be published on this site as sessions are finalised.

Expected outcomes

Near term: workshop proceedings and materials, and a dedicated site for presentations and resources; knowledge transfer to extension networks.

Longer term: peer-reviewed outputs, policy-relevant recommendations, and frameworks for sustained collaboration—including US–Israel research partnerships supported by BARD.
